This principle focuses on responsible campfire practices to reduce the environmental and ecological impact of fires in the outdoors. Campfires can cause long-lasting damage to ecosystems, so this principle provides guidance on when and how to have fires when permitted.



Overview

Keep Fires Small. If you must have a fire, keep it small. A smaller fire is easier to manage, uses fewer resources, and minimizes the potential for sparks or embers that could start a wildfire.

Use a Camp Stove. Whenever possible, use a camp stove for cooking and heating instead of making an open fire. Camp stoves are more efficient, leave no fire scars, and produce less smoke and pollution.

Use Established Fire Rings or Pans. If fires are allowed and there are established fire rings or fire pans at your campsite, use them. These designated areas are designed to minimize the impact of fires on the environment.

Respect Fire Bans and Regulations. If a fire ban is in place or fires are prohibited due to environmental concerns, respect these restrictions. This helps prevent wildfires and environmental damage.


Why is this important?

Minimizing campfire impact contributes to the long-term sustainability of outdoor spaces. By avoiding damage from fires, these areas can continue to provide recreation and inspiration for future generations.


What does this look like?

Scenario: A group of campers plan a trip to a forested area known for its susceptibility to wildfires. Knowing the environmental risks, they choose to opt out of campfires altogether, relying instead on camp stoves for cooking and heating.

grade Importance: By respecting local fire bans and regulations, these campers help prevent wildfires, protect the forest, and ensure the safety of the environment and themselves.
